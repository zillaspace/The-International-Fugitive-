The Story

Anthony Marinello, a 28-year New Jersey State Police veteran and career helicopter rescue pilot, bought a small island charter company, discovered there was no helicopter air ambulance serving the Bahamas, and built one from scratch. His very first call wasn't a Bahamian medical emergency. It was a request from a U.S. congressman to fly into the collapsing nation of Haiti, at night, through five countries, with no support, to extract 10 trapped Americans from a war zone. He said yes. He was the 13th company called. The other 12 said no.

The Stakes

Why This Story Matters

In the Bahamas, someone has a stroke on the wrong island, and geography becomes the enemy. A shark bite on a remote cay. A cardiac event on a private yacht. A boating accident where the nearest hospital is hours away by boat and there is no helicopter coming. Before Tropic Air Rescue, the answer was a fixed-wing air ambulance that could take 8 to 36 hours to arrive, if it came at all. Patients had to be transported to an airport. Payment had to be arranged in advance. Insurance had to be confirmed. And the clock kept running.

Marinello's service exists to compress that geography. Sikorsky S-76 helicopters launch directly from Fort Lauderdale Executive Airport, staffed with certified trauma paramedics and nurses, carrying whole blood, defibrillators, and critical care equipment. A patient can be back in a U.S. trauma center within a couple of hours of the call. But between the call and the landing, the real story unfolds: a gauntlet of open water, weather, international borders, fragmented island governance, insurance obstacles, and a system that was never built for speed.

"The water between is the story."

This is about who gets saved, how fast, and at what cost when the map is working against you. It is a story about building a new infrastructure of survival in a geography that has never had one.

The Full Picture

Story Overview

Anthony Marinello spent 28 years in the New Jersey State Police, 21 of them as a helicopter pilot. He flew the first medevac of New Jersey's statewide helicopter medical response program on July 1, 1988. He flew governors and chased fugitives and pulled people out of wreckage for decades. He appeared in HBO's Succession as a helicopter pilot. By 2021, he was done working for other people.

He found a small FAA Part 135 charter company, Tropic Air Charters, that had been flying passengers and cargo between Florida and the Bahamas for 30 years. The owner wanted to sell but got cold feet. Marinello almost walked. COVID hit. The deal stalled, restarted, nearly died, and then closed. Marinello spent six months learning the business from the inside, sweeping floors and washing airplanes while the staff thought he was just a helpful friend. Then he bought it.

Within months, he expanded the fleet by 50%. Things were running well. Then one of his pilots called from a remote Bahamian island with a passenger having a stroke. No hospital. No ambulance. No helicopter. Marinello called 10 to 15 air ambulance companies. Most said no. One said it could get there by Sunday. It was Thursday. He found a private plane and got the man out on his own.

"I spent the next day researching the closest helicopter air ambulance in the Bahamas. Turned out there isn't one. There was never one."

The next morning, he told his VP they were changing the direction of the company. Two years of aircraft acquisition, FAA certification (the hardest designation to obtain, by his account), crew hiring, medical protocol development, and state licensing later, Tropic Air Rescue launched on March 1, 2024, as the first FAA-certified helicopter air ambulance dedicated to serving the Bahamas.

The phone didn't ring. For ten days, nothing. On March 10, at 7:30 PM, the call came. Not from the Bahamas. From a U.S. congressman's office. Ten Americans were trapped in an orphanage in Port-au-Prince, Haiti. The country was in civil war. Could he get them out?

Marinello was the 13th company called. The other 12 said no. He said yes.

Mission Route — Haiti Extraction, March 2024
Fort Lauderdale → Nassau → Turks & Caicos → Puerto Plata → Santo Domingo → Port-au-Prince → Barahona, DR

He stripped the S-76 down to save weight, calculated fuel loads to the gallon, flew through the night across five countries with a congressman and a Delta Force operator in the back, navigated mountain passes using a topography map on an iPad, and descended into a burning city at 3 AM. The people in the orphanage blinked the lights on and off. He found them. He landed. Sixty-seven seconds on the ground. Everyone out. Over the trees, into the clouds, gone.

When they landed in the Dominican Republic at 4 AM, 12 people poured out of a helicopter at a closed airport. Immigration was not expecting them. The Haitian Prime Minister resigned. Marinello learned the flyby of his stars-and-bars-marked helicopter had been mistaken for a U.S. military incursion.

He went back. Multiple times. Contracted by the Florida Division of Emergency Management, he led a multi-helicopter rescue task force that ultimately extracted close to 700 Americans from Haiti. The Haitian authorities demanded his arrest as an international fugitive. The Dominican Republic refused to honor the request. The name stuck.

Back in the Bahamas, the real mission continues. Shark bites, boating accidents, strokes, cardiac events, golf cart crashes, yacht emergencies. Over 50 rescues in the first year. A crew of 30 full-time and 18 part-time employees. The 2024 Sikorsky Rescue Award. And a vision to expand down the entire Caribbean chain.

Founder / President / Operator-Builder

Marinello is not a boardroom executive who funded a helicopter company. He is the helicopter company. He owns it, flies it, built it, certified it, and personally piloted the most dangerous rescue mission in its history. That combination, field-credible operational leader with entrepreneurial vision, is what makes him both a documentary subject and a feature protagonist.

28 years New Jersey State Police. 21 years as a helicopter pilot. Flew the first medevac of New Jersey's statewide helicopter medical response program (JEMSTAR), July 1, 1988. Approximately 5,000 rescues over his career. Over 14,000 flight hours. Former chief pilot, Newark Police Department. Served as township councilman, deputy mayor, and mayor in his New Jersey hometown. Held various aviation leadership roles at CAE, HeliFlite, and other operators. Purchased Tropic Air Charters in 2021. Founded Tropic Air Rescue in 2023. Recipient of the 2024 Sikorsky Rescue Award and New Jersey State Trooper of the Year.

He appeared in HBO's Succession as a helicopter pilot. He was driving to work at 8 AM when the rescue permit for Haiti arrived in his email and immediately turned the helicopter south. He bribed Turks and Caicos ground staff $20,000 to keep the airport open at 3 AM. He told his co-pilot: "Under no circumstances will we be captured. If I get killed, just keep flying."

This is a serious cinematic figure. Not a generic executive.

Dramatic Forces

The Antagonists

The antagonists of this story are not villains. They are forces. The thing that makes this material cinematic is that the danger is systemic, distributed, and relentless. There is no single bad guy. There is a network of obstacles that conspire against every rescue.

Force
The Sea
Open water between Florida and the Bahamas. No emergency landing zones. Engine failure over blue water at night is fatal. The ocean is indifferent and absolute.
Force
Weather
Tropical storms, low cloud ceilings, unpredictable squalls. A 500-foot ceiling and three miles visibility over an unfamiliar coastline at night. Weather doesn't negotiate.
Force
Time
Every minute over open water changes the outcome. A stroke patient loses brain tissue by the hour. A trauma patient bleeds. The clock is the villain that never stops.
Force
Distance
700 islands spread across 100,000 square miles of ocean. The wrong island can be the difference between life and death. Geography is the original antagonist.
Force
Fragmented Island Governance
No one cohesive group to speak to. Island-by-island variability in medical infrastructure, airfield access, and emergency coordination. Systems built for a world before helicopter rescue existed.
Force
Legacy Fixed-Wing Culture
Islands that have only ever known one method: get the patient to the airport. A helicopter that can land next to the scene is a concept they have to be educated on. Old systems resist new capability.
Force
Border-Entry Rules & Bureaucracy
Customs, immigration, general declarations, rescue permits that may or may not exist. You land at a closed airport at 4 AM with 12 people who didn't enter through normal channels and the authorities want answers. Paperwork doesn't care that someone is dying.
Force
Insurance & Reimbursement
Air ambulance companies that demand payment before dispatch. Insurance that doesn't cover international helicopter transport. The cost of readiness is enormous, but the cost of being uncovered is worse.

Operational Reality

Systems Friction by Jurisdiction

The Bahamas

Multi-agency complexity across a fragmented archipelago. No centralized emergency medical dispatch for helicopter rescue. Island-by-island variability in airfield infrastructure, medical resources, and communications. Many islands have never received a helicopter air ambulance flight. The existing system is built around fixed-wing transport to airports, which can add hours to a rescue that a direct helicopter landing could solve in minutes. Limited healthcare infrastructure on smaller islands. Reimbursement models designed for a different era of patient transport. Educating local authorities, homeowners associations, and medical providers about helicopter capability is an ongoing process that runs in parallel with actual rescue operations.

The United States

FAA certification for helicopter air ambulance operations is, by Marinello's account, the hardest aspect to obtain. COPCN (Certificate of Public Convenience and Necessity) laws in Florida prevent private helicopter ambulances from responding to emergency scenes within the state, as counties hold those designations for their own EMS programs. Cross-border re-entry with medically urgent patients creates documentation challenges. Insurance and medical eligibility create friction at the receiving end. Aviation regulatory burden in cross-border operations adds layers of compliance to every flight.

Third-Country / International

Patients who are neither U.S. citizens nor Bahamian nationals create a third layer of complexity: entry documentation, visa status, passport validity, insurance coverage, and medical admissibility. In the Haiti operation, the challenge was extracting Americans from a country with a collapsing government, transiting through the Dominican Republic, and getting them processed through immigration at closed airports in the middle of the night. Rescue permits were invented on the fly. The international dimension is not an edge case. It is a recurring operational reality.

Market Context

Why Now

Audiences are responding to real operational stories with mission structure and procedural credibility. Premium rescue documentaries and docuseries are performing across platforms. Cross-border dysfunction, healthcare access, and emergency infrastructure are resonant contemporary themes. The contrast between paradise and emergency is one of the strongest visual and emotional hooks available: the same turquoise water that sells vacation packages becomes a life-threatening obstacle when someone has a cardiac event on the wrong island.

Short-form and long-form platforms both reward mission-based storytelling with recurring casts and built-in stakes. Aviation content continues to perform well across digital audiences. The geopolitical dimension (Haiti, border sovereignty, fragmented governance) adds depth and urgency beyond what a domestic rescue story can achieve. And the founder is alive, active, operational, and available, a rare alignment of access and dramatic material.

"Paradise can become a trap in a single phone call."

Format Potential

Scripted Feature: The International Fugitive

The title works because it gives the material scale, urgency, and thriller energy. It suggests the protagonist is crossing systems, not just crossing water. The fictionalized version should feel like a cross-border rescue thriller grounded in real operational truth, where the protagonist's act of saving lives puts him in direct conflict with the machinery of international governance.

The core tonal idea: saving a life starts to resemble an illicit transfer. A man flies into a collapsing nation at night without authorization, extracts civilians, crosses multiple borders, lands at a closed airport, and is declared a fugitive by a foreign government. He does it again. And again. The title isn't metaphorical. It's what they called him.

The scripted version amplifies the cross-border friction, deepens the antagonist systems, and builds a compressed thriller structure around the real operational world. Helicopter cinematography, open-water tension, cockpit instrumentation, night flying, and the bureaucratic gauntlet create a visual and procedural texture that is rare in the genre.

Two-Paragraph High-Power Pitch

In the Bahamas, 700 islands share three hospitals and, until recently, zero helicopter ambulances. When someone has a stroke on the wrong island, a shark bite on a remote cay, or a cardiac event on a private yacht, geography becomes the enemy. The nearest trauma center can be hours away by boat, and fixed-wing air ambulances can take a day or more to arrive, if they come at all. Tropic Air Rescue, founded by 28-year New Jersey State Police veteran and career helicopter pilot Anthony Marinello, exists to compress that geography: Sikorsky S-76 helicopters, critical care crews, 24/7 readiness, and the operational nerve to fly where others won't.

But the bigger story is what happens when saving a life means crossing borders, breaking rules, and fighting systems that were never built for speed. Marinello's very first call wasn't a Bahamian medical emergency. It was a request to fly into a collapsing Haiti at night, rescue 10 trapped Americans, and get them out alive. He was the 13th company called. The other 12 said no. He said yes, flew through five countries with a congressman and a Delta Force operator, extracted the civilians in 67 seconds, and was declared an international fugitive by the Haitian government. Then he went back and rescued 700 more. The material spans commercial scripted feature, premium documentary, docuseries, and digital short series, with an extraordinary visual world, a field-credible protagonist, and a story that sits at the intersection of rescue thriller, systems drama, and modern frontier narrative.
